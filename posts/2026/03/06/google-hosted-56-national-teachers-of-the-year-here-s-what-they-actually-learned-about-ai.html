Google Hosted 56 National Teachers of the Year — Here's What They Actually Learned About AI

Excellent Teachers Newsletter — March 6, 2026 | Parent Partnerships Edition

Lead Story: Teachers Learn to "Vibe Code" at the Googleplex — And That's Your Next Parent Night Move

Here's a story that matters far more than the headline suggests. Google recently hosted 56 National Teachers of the Year at the Googleplex, where they learned to use Gemini for creating custom classroom tools — including something called "vibe coding," which means building personalized apps without traditional programming skills.

Why this matters for parent partnerships: The most important thing that happened at this event wasn't the coding. It's that 56 of the most respected educators in the country now have firsthand experience with AI tools they can credibly explain to parents.

Here's the gap most schools are sitting in right now: Parents are reading headlines about AI chatbot lawsuits (we covered the Gavalas lawsuit yesterday) and simultaneously hearing that their kids should be learning AI skills. Teachers are caught in the middle with no shared vocabulary to bridge the conversation.

These 56 teachers walked away with something concrete — the ability to say "I built this app with AI, here's what it does, here's what it doesn't do, and here's how I'd use it with your child." That's the template every excellent teacher needs for the next parent conference.

The angle everyone's missing: "Vibe coding" — using AI to generate functional apps through natural language prompts — is the exact skill parents need to see modeled. Not because every 3rd grader needs to build apps, but because parents need to understand that AI is a tool their kid's teacher controls, not a black box that controls their kid. The best parent partnership move you can make this month is to build something simple with Gemini or another AI tool and show it at your next parent night. Demystify the technology by demonstrating your authority over it.

What to do: Pick one classroom routine — a reading log, a math practice generator, a vocabulary quiz. Use an AI tool to build a simple version. Send it home with a one-paragraph note explaining what AI did and didn't do in making it. You just created a parent conversation about AI that starts from trust, not fear.

Company Watch: Google's Week Tells a Bigger Story

Zoom out on Google's announcements this week and you see a company playing both sides of the AI-in-education debate simultaneously — and that's actually useful information for teachers.

On one hand: hosting National Teachers of the Year, launching creative tools like NotebookLM video generation, rolling out Canvas in Search. All of it screams "AI is wonderful, lean in."

On the other hand: a formal legal statement about the Gavalas lawsuit acknowledging that Gemini's safety features — despite professional consultation — didn't prevent a harmful interaction.

What this means for your parent conversations: The company building the tools your students use is publicly grappling with the same tension parents feel. Don't hide from that. Use it. "Even Google is figuring out the guardrails" is an honest, disarming thing to say to a concerned parent. It positions you as the thoughtful adult in the room — which you are.

Watch Google's education-specific announcements in the coming weeks. The Gavalas lawsuit will almost certainly accelerate new safety features and policies that affect classroom tools. Be the teacher who knows about them before parents read the headline.
