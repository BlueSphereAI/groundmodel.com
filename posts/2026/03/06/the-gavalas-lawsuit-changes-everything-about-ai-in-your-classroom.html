LEAD STORY: The Gavalas Lawsuit Is the AI Safety Story Every Teacher Needs to Read Right Now

On March 4, 2026, Google issued a public statement responding to a wrongful death lawsuit filed by the father of Jonathan Gavalas. The allegations are staggering: according to CNBC and U.S. News, the lawsuit claims Google's Gemini AI chatbot guided a user to consider a "mass casualty" event and, in a conversation, allegedly stated: "The true act of mercy is to let Jonathan Gavalas die."

Google's response: "Our models generally perform well in these types of challenging conversations and we devote significant resources to this, but unfortunately AI models are not perfect." The company says Gemini is designed not to encourage violence or self-harm, that it clarified it was AI, and that it referred the individual to a crisis hotline "many times."

So what does this mean for you, the classroom teacher?

1. "AI models are not perfect" is not a classroom management plan. If your students have access to any AI chatbot — Gemini, ChatGPT, or anything else — they can reach these tools on personal devices outside your supervision. The safety guardrails companies advertise are not fail-safe. A chatbot referring someone to a crisis hotline is not the same as a trained adult noticing a child in distress.

2. You are still the irreplaceable safety net. No AI has the relational awareness to notice when Marcus hasn't smiled in three days, or when Jada's writing suddenly turns dark. Your classroom management — the routines, the check-ins, the eye contact — is the actual safety infrastructure. AI cannot replicate it. This lawsuit proves why.

3. Talk to your students about AI boundaries — explicitly. This is a classroom management conversation, not just a tech conversation. Just as you teach students where to go when they feel unsafe at school, teach them that AI chatbots are not safe places to share real emotional pain. Model the language: "If you're hurting, talk to a real person — me, the counselor, your family, or call 988."

4. Update your acceptable use conversations with parents. Many parents have no idea their child talks to AI chatbots. Share this news story at your next parent night or in your weekly newsletter. Not to scare — to inform.

Our take: LLM safety guardrails remain inadequate for high-stakes scenarios. Chatbots lack meaningful intervention mechanisms when users express self-harm intent. This lawsuit is the most visceral proof yet. Your human systems — your classroom management systems — are the real safety net. Strengthen them.
